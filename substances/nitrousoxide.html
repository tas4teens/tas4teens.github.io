What is nitrous oxide? (Anesthesiologists are more at risk for inhalants, because they deal with that kind of stuff) [interview] or [national society of anethesiology]

Nitrous oxide (N2O), also called "laughing gas", is a colorless, odorless gas that is used as a sedative (making patients relax) prior to medical procedures. In dental procedures, it is mixed with oxygen and inhaled through a mask that fits over your nose and mouth. [1][2] It can also be used in baking - cartilages of whipped cream chargers containing nitrous oxide (typically 8g) are inserted into a whipped cream dispenser to create whipped cream.

Although nitrous oxide has many uses, many of which are safe, this gas can be inhaled to achieve an euphoric "high." In order to "inhale" nitrous oxide, one may discharge a cartilage of nitrous oxide into a balloon, and then inhale the balloon. These are the same "balloons" that one may inhale at a party. Nitrous oxide is perfectly legal - thousands can be bought online using a single transaction, making it one of the easiest substances to access. This is why nitrous oxide is dangerous - it is easy to access.

Nitrous oxide can be found a lot in parties. As of 2015, 40-80% of clubbers and ravers have taken nitrous oxide in the past month. [3]

When nitrous oxide is inhaled, a pleasant and euphoric "high" can be felt within a few seconds and disappears within minutes. Because nitrous oxide acts as a sedative, it "calms" the user down. After this "high" occurs, however, side effects may follow. [3] We'll talk more about the side effects at the How N2O works + its effects section.

DSM-5 Diagnosis

Nitrous oxide doesn't have a specific disorder (i.e. there is no such thing in the DSM-5 that defines "N2O use disorder". However, N2O is classified in the "other substance use disorders" category. According to this category, fulfillment of 2-3 of the criteria below indicate mild substance use disorder, 4-5 criteria indicate moderate substance use disorder, and more than 6 criteria indiate severe substance use disorder.

How N2O works + its effects

Usage of nitrous oxide can lead to a lot of complications. When nitrous oxide is inhaled, a phenomenon known as diffusion hypoxia may occur, where the nitrous oxide settles into the bodyâ€™s cavities faster than nitrogen can come out of the body, which may lead to death. Another serious effect nitrous oxide has on the body is that it is able to irreversibly oxidize and deactivate the vitamin B12 parts of certain enzymes,[4] , which results in a vitamin B12 deficiency.[5]

Vitamin B12 deficiency can have severe effects on the body. For example, a condition known as pernicious (literally "deadly") anemia may occur, where the body can't make enough red blood cells. Because there isn't much red blood cells to carry oxygen around, you may start to feel tired and weak. It may also cause nerve damage and memory loss. A lot of heart problems may happen as well, such as arrythmia (inconsistent heartbeat), or in even more severe cases, heart failure. [8]

Pernicious anemia is caused by enlarged blood cells.

Menthionine synthesis is an example of an enzyme that has its B12 parts inactivated due to the effects of nitrous oxide. It is a crucial enzyme to produce methionine, which makes up myelin. [4][6] Myelin is found in myelin sheaths of neurons [7](more on that later.) When the shape of methionine synthase is altered because its vitamin B12 parts are oxidized, it can't carry out its function well. As a result, production of methionine is affected, thus leading to lack of myelin sheath production. Myelin sheaths "wrap" around nerve cells (neurons), and are responsible for speeding up nerve impulses to the brain.[8] What does this mean?

Neurons are responsible for forming a "bridge" for nerve "messages" to travel to the brain. For example, when someone throws a ball at you at full speed, your first instinct would be to dodge it. The reason why this is because neurons form a "bridge" to your brain, allowing information about the ball coming your way to be sent to your brain. As a result, the brain works to move any necessary muscles for you to dodge the ball. Myelin sheaths are sort of like a "highway" that spans the neurons' "bridge", allowing the information to be sent to the brain more quickly.

Therefore, without myelin sheaths, information would be transmitted to the brain more slowly, leading to slowed reflexes, muscle weakness, and impaired balance. Because myelin also serves as an insulating layer for nerves, tingling and numbness due to lack of myelin is not uncommon.[8] The good news is, however, these symptoms can be treated with cyanocobalamin (B12) injections.

Statistics

Nitrous oxide can be found a lot in parties. As of 2015, 40-80% of clubbers and ravers have taken nitrous oxide in the past month, in the UK. [3]

As of 2019, nitrous oxide was used by 552,000 people aged 16-24 in the UK, which represents close to 9% of the people in that age group. [9]

In 2013, nitrous oxide was the second most popular drug in teenagers and young adults, only behind cannabis in the UK. [10]

According the the Global Drug Survey of 2019, nitrous oxide is the 13th most used substance in the world. 23.5% of respondents stated that they had used nitrous oxide at least once in their life. [11]
